Who I am

I'm a first year PhD student in the Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering at MIT. As a member of the Howland Lab, I study the intersection of environmental fluid mechanics, atmospheric modeling, uncertainty quantification, and optimization. My current area of focus is wind energy performance due to atmospheric boundary layer effects. My research is supported by a Department of Energy Computational Science Graduate Fellowship (DOE CSGF).

I graduated from Dartmouth College in 2019 with an A.B. in Engineering Sciences where I was advised by Prof. Benoit Cushman-Roisin. I was also the Sustainability and Engineering Program Assistant in the Division of Engineering and Utilities where I assisted with the ongoing steam-to-hot-water conversion of the campus energy network.

What I do

My work focuses broadly on wind energy optimization and resilience to climate change. My current research is spread among three areas:

1) developing improved wind turbine power modeling methods and examining the atmospheric determinants of power output variability,

2) using climate modeling to determine potential effects of climate change on future wind generation, and

3) developing a framework to guide siting and design of future wind farm projects to maximize performance.
